# Analysis and Revision

## Diagnosis

The passage has one central problem: it narrates emotion instead of creating the conditions for the reader to feel it. Every sentence either names an emotional state directly ("feeling deeply sad," "heartbroken," "even more depressed," "profound sense of loss," "sorrow and regret," "emptiness," "overwhelming") or explains what Margaret is thinking in language that summarizes rather than renders. The reader is told what to feel at every turn and given nothing to discover.

Specific issues:

1. **Emotional over-direction throughout.** The passage uses at least seven explicit emotion-labels in eight sentences. Each one closes off the scene a little more. By the time we reach "The emptiness inside her was overwhelming," there is nothing left for the reader to interpret --- every feeling has been pre-digested.

2. **The cold coffee is wasted.** This is the single concrete, physical detail in the passage, and it could do real work. Instead, the text immediately explains its emotional significance ("which made her even more depressed"), converting a living image into another label.

3. **Dialogue does nothing the narration hasn't already done.** "I guess this is it" could be a strong moment --- understated, final. But it is surrounded by explicit narration of "sorrow and regret," so it has no room to resonate on its own.

4. **The final two sentences collapse the future.** "She knew she would never be the same again" and "she wondered if she would ever feel happy again" are the kind of summary statements that sound conclusive but actually flatten the character into a generic outline of grief. They tell us Margaret is sad in the way any person might be sad. They do not show us how *this* person, in *this* kitchen, is coming apart.

## Revision A: Restrained, mostly external

> Margaret pressed her thumb against the rim of the coffee mug and held it there. The coffee had gone cold sometime in the last hour --- she wasn't sure when. She'd noticed the film forming on the surface and had watched it without doing anything about it.
>
> The kitchen faucet dripped. She'd been meaning to call someone about that. They'd both been meaning to.
>
> "I guess this is it," she said, to no one.
>
> She picked up the mug, carried it to the sink, and poured it out.

**What this gains:** The emotion is carried entirely by objects and actions. The cold coffee now does real work --- Margaret watching the film form on its surface tells us she's been sitting motionless, unable to act, without the passage ever needing to say so. The faucet drip and the shift to "they'd both been meaning to" opens the marriage without explaining it. The final gesture --- pouring out the coffee --- is small and physical but carries the weight of finality. The reader arrives at the grief rather than being told about it.

## Revision B: Slightly more interior access, still controlled

> Margaret sat at the kitchen table with her hands around a mug that had gone cold. She could feel the ceramic pulling heat from her palms.
>
> Fourteen years. She kept catching on that number. Not on the arguments or the silence that replaced them, but on the sheer amount of ordinary time --- the Tuesday grocery runs, the way he set the thermostat two degrees lower than she wanted, the things that were only visible now that they were about to stop.
>
> "I guess this is it," she said.
>
> The kitchen didn't answer. She hadn't expected it to.

**What this gains:** This version allows more interior access but controls it carefully. Instead of naming the emotion ("profound sense of loss"), it renders the specific texture of what Margaret is actually thinking about --- not grief in the abstract, but grocery runs and thermostat settings. The mundane details do more to convey what is being lost than any amount of explicit sorrow-language. "The kitchen didn't answer" keeps the tone dry and contained, which paradoxically makes the moment land harder. The restraint signals that the grief is too large for the prose to match directly.

## The principle at work

The original tells the reader "Margaret is devastated" seven different ways. The revisions create a space in which the reader *feels* that Margaret is devastated, without ever being instructed to. This is the difference between explaining an emotion and earning it. Every named feeling ("heartbroken," "overwhelming emptiness") is a door the writer closes. Every concrete, unexplained detail --- cold coffee, a dripping faucet, a thermostat set two degrees too low --- is a door the writer leaves open for the reader to walk through.
